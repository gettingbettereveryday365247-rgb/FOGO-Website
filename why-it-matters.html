The bigger picture

Why a lime-green bin makes a real difference

It's not just tidiness. Separating food and garden waste changes what happens to it chemically, and that has consequences for climate, landfill space, and soil.

Three reasons

What changes when scraps are sorted

Food and garden waste behaves very differently depending on where it ends up. Here's what separating it actually achieves.

🌫️

Less methane

Buried under other rubbish, food waste breaks down without oxygen and produces methane — a greenhouse gas many times more potent than CO₂ over the short term. Composting is an aerobic process, so it largely avoids this.

Source: Sustainability Victoria, Clean Air Council
🏔️

Landfill lasts longer

Food and garden material makes up a large share of general waste bin weight. Diverting it significantly extends the life of existing landfill sites and delays the need for new ones.

Source: Victorian council waste audits
🌱

Healthier soil

Composted organics return nutrients and structure to soil — supporting farms, parks and gardens instead of sitting inert underground for decades.

Source: EPA Victoria compost standards

Same waste, two very different outcomes

In a landfill, food scraps are compacted and buried, so they decompose anaerobically (without oxygen) — this is the pathway that generates methane.

In a composting facility, the same scraps are kept aerated and turned regularly, so they break down aerobically — releasing mostly CO₂ and water vapour, and finishing as usable compost rather than buried waste.
