Kerb to compost

The full journey of your FOGO bin

From your kitchen caddy to finished compost takes about eight weeks. Here's exactly what happens at each stage.

Step by step

Where it goes after collection

Councils vary slightly in collection frequency and processing partners, but the core process is the same everywhere FOGO operates.

Day 0
🪣

Kitchen caddy

Scraps collect in a small benchtop caddy — no liner needed, or a council-approved one only, depending on local rules.

Day 0–7
🗑️

Outdoor bin

Caddy empties into the lime-green FOGO bin along with garden clippings, ready for kerbside collection on your council's schedule.

Collection day
🚛

Kerbside collection

A dedicated truck collects FOGO separately from general waste and recycling, taking it directly to a licensed organics processing facility.

Week 1
⚙️

Shredding & mixing

Material is shredded and blended to balance moisture and carbon-to-nitrogen ratio, which helps it break down evenly.

Weeks 2–6
🌡️

Active composting

Piles are aerated and regularly turned. Internal temperatures rise well above body temperature, which breaks down material fast and destroys weed seeds and pathogens.

Weeks 6–8
🔍

Curing & screening

The compost cures and is screened to remove any contaminants (like stray plastic) and achieve a fine, even texture.

Week 8+
🌱

Back to the land

Finished compost is supplied to farms, vineyards, parks and landscaping — closing the loop back into soil.

Common questions

FAQ

Do I need a compostable liner? +

It depends on your council. Some accept certified compostable liners (look for the AS 4736 standard), others prefer no liner at all since caddies can be rinsed. Check your council's website — using the wrong liner is a common cause of contamination.

What happens if I put the wrong thing in? +

A small amount is usually screened out during processing. But too much contamination — especially plastic — can cause an entire truckload to be rejected and sent to landfill instead, which is why sorting carefully matters.

Can I compost at home instead? +

Yes, and home composting is great for fruit and veg scraps. But most home systems can't safely handle meat, bones, dairy or large volumes of garden waste — the FOGO bin covers everything home compost can't.

Where does the finished compost actually go? +

It's typically sold or supplied to agriculture, viticulture, landscaping and council parks programs — genuinely returning to the land rather than sitting in landfill.
